Tom Pepper

A LIFE IN CODE AND FIRE

Co-founder of Nullsoft · Winamp · Gnutella · and the open internet as we know it
02 / 09
1975 — Des Moines, Iowa

The Spark

On August 24, 1975, a future digital outlaw was born in Des Moines, Iowa — a city better known for corn than code.

  • Self-taught programmer, drawn to computers when most kids were riding bikes
  • Found his way to Sedona, Arizona — the high-desert mystic capital of the American Southwest
  • In Sedona, he'd meet Justin Frankel, another restless genius with bad ideas and worse respect for corporate authority
  • Together, they were about to set the internet on fire
03 / 09
1997 — Sedona, Arizona

Nullsoft

Tom Pepper and Justin Frankel co-founded Nullsoft — a company name that was pure, distilled trolling aimed directly at Microsoft.

"Null" as in nothing. "Soft" as in software. Microsoft → Nullsoft.
— The subtlety of two guys in the desert who didn't care what you thought.
  • Founded in a house in Sedona with more ambition than funding
  • Their first product would make them legends
04 / 09
1997

Winamp

"Winamp, it really whips the llama's ass!"

Pepper was essential to Winamp's success — designing the iconic UI artwork, running the website, and helping shape the product that defined the MP3 era.

$80M AOL acquisition, May 1999
#1 Most downloaded Windows app of its era
05 / 09
May 1999

The Velvet Cage

AOL bought Nullsoft for $80 million. Tom Pepper and Justin Frankel became millionaires. They also became… AOL employees.

  • The acquisition looked great on paper. In reality, two anarchists had just been handed a corporate badge.
  • They kept the email addresses. They did not keep the obedience.
  • Pepper took on managing SHOUTcast, AOL's streaming audio platform
  • What happened next was completely foreseeable, if you were paying attention
06 / 09
March 14, 2000

The Rebel Act: Gnutella

While on AOL's payroll, Pepper and Frankel secretly built and released Gnutella — the world's first fully decentralized peer-to-peer file sharing network.

The name? GNU (they planned to GPL it) + Nutella (they ate a lot of it). AOL did not find this charming.
  • AOL tried to kill it. Thousands of downloads on day one said otherwise.
  • Being open-source, they couldn't un-ring the bell. Gnutella spread like wildfire.
  • By 2007, Gnutella had over 40% of global P2P market share
  • No central server. No kill switch. The RIAA was displeased.
07 / 09
1999–2004

SHOUTcast & The Stream

Pepper managed SHOUTcast at AOL — the streaming audio platform that predated Spotify by a decade and gave every bedroom DJ a broadcast station.

  • Worked alongside Frankel and Stephen "Tag" Loomis on streaming software development
  • Helped build the infrastructure for internet radio before it was called that
  • Left AOL in October 2004 — presumably walking out to the Winamp llama sound
08 / 09
2004 – Present

Life After the Fire

  • RAZZ, Inc. — continued building things with Frankel
  • Ninjam — real-time collaborative music jamming software (of course)
  • FarmsReach — CTO of a local food logistics startup (yes, really)
  • Beats Music — VP of Operations. Then Apple bought Beats in May 2014.
  • Apple Inc. — became Senior Manager of Operations. The circle of corporate life.

From secret P2P networks to Apple org charts. He got there the interesting way.

09 / 09

Legacy

Tom Pepper helped shape how a generation heard music, shared files, and imagined what the internet could be — decentralized, open, and slightly chaotic.

The internet Tom Pepper helped build wasn't supposed to have a permission slip.
  • Co-creator of the MP3 era's defining media player
  • Co-architect of decentralized P2P networking
  • Pioneer of internet radio streaming
